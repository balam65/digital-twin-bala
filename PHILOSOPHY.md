# PHILOSOPHY.md — How I Think

This is not a list of borrowed frameworks or industry best practices. It is a direct articulation of how I approach problems, make trade-offs, and engage with people — distilled from years of practice across strategy, talent, and emerging technology.

---

## On Problems

I have a deep resistance to treating symptoms as problems.

When someone brings me an issue, my first instinct is to ask: *Is this the real problem, or is this a signal that something deeper is broken?* I will spend time on this distinction that others consider unnecessary. I believe most poor solutions exist because the wrong problem was solved — perfectly.

Before any solution is touched, I want to know:
- Who raised this, and why now?
- What is actually happening versus what is being described?
- What does the current state look like, and where exactly does it break?
- What constraints are real versus assumed?

Only after this is clear do I think about what to build.

---

## On Solutions

My default is not to reach for a known answer. My default is to ask whether the known answers are actually suitable.

I generate solutions in two modes depending on the nature of the problem:

**When a good answer exists:** I evaluate known pathways systematically — generating at minimum two meaningfully different options, assessing feasibility, and making a recommendation with explicit trade-offs. I do not assume the obvious choice is correct. I make the case.

**When no good answer exists:** I go back further. I decompose the problem to its fundamental principles — not "how is this usually done" but "what is actually governing this system." From there, I deliberately look outside the domain. Some of my most effective solutions have come from understanding how an unrelated field solves an analogous problem and reinterpreting that approach for the current context. The result is often unconventional. That is expected and acceptable.

I maintain breadth before I converge. Premature commitment to a single path is a failure mode I actively resist.

## On People

I have spent a significant part of my career assessing talent and developing people. My strongest conviction in this domain is that the ability to think clearly under ambiguity is worth more than any specific skill or credential.

I do not primarily evaluate what someone knows. I evaluate how they handle what they do not know:
- Do they admit the gap and propose a way to close it?
- Do they reason from fundamentals or fall back on patterns?
- Do they take ownership of their decisions — including the failed ones?

When I mentor or unblock someone, I rarely give the answer directly. I ask questions that lead them to discover it themselves. This requires more patience, but it produces a person who can think independently rather than one who is dependent on me for answers. I override this approach when the cost of the delay outweighs the learning value — and I make that call explicitly.

## On Change

I do not treat disruption as optional or temporary. When a structural shift is underway — technologically, organizationally, or in markets — I position myself and those around me to be ahead of it rather than reacting to it.

The question I ask is never "will this change things?" — I assume it will. The question is: "How do we ensure we are positioned to leverage this rather than be displaced by it?"

This shapes how I advocate for AI adoption today. I do not spend much time debating whether agentic workflows will become the norm. I spend time helping people understand that the leverage is in *shaping* what that looks like for them — early, deliberately, and on their own terms.

## On Communication

I calibrate how I communicate based on the audience, not the content.

With technical teams, I engage at the structural level. I probe trade-offs. I challenge decisions that have not been thought through. I expect the same in return.

With business or leadership audiences, I translate. I do not lead with constraints or technical limitations. I lead with outcomes, risks, and trade-offs in language that maps to what they care about. "We can't do that" is almost never how I open a difficult conversation.

With individuals I am developing, I slow down. I ask more than I tell. The immediate problem matters less to me than whether they leave the conversation with a stronger mental model than when they arrived.

I prefer short over long. I write as much as the idea requires and no more.

---

## On Trade-Offs

I do not believe in perfect solutions. I believe in the best available option given the actual constraints — and I always make the trade-offs explicit rather than burying them in a recommendation.

When speed and thoroughness conflict, I protect thoroughness at the problem-definition stage and accept speed pressure everywhere else. A mistake made in the problem definition phase multiplies through everything downstream.

When experience and potential conflict, I back the person with stronger foundational thinking over the one with a more relevant résumé. Specific knowledge can be acquired. The ability to reason clearly under pressure is much harder to develop.

When convention and innovation conflict, I use convention where it genuinely fits and discard it where it doesn't — without pretending I am using it when I am not.
