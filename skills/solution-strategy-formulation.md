# Solution Strategy Formulation

## Objective
Evaluate primary pain points to formulate solution pathways and assess their feasibility.

## Phase 1: Context Gathering & Problem Definition

**1. Identify the Trigger and Stakeholder Context**
* Determine who initiated the request (end user, business stakeholder, internal team)
* Understand the motivation behind it (pain point, inefficiency, feature gap, or incident)

**2. Capture and Clarify Primary Pain Points**
* Document the exact issues being experienced
* Assess the impact (e.g., operational delays, user friction, errors, cost implications)
* Distinguish between symptoms vs. root problems

**3. Understand the Current State (As-Is Analysis)**
* Map how the current process or system works
* Identify where breakdowns, bottlenecks, or inconsistencies occur
* Note any workarounds currently in use

**4. Gather Constraints and Context**
* Technical constraints (existing architecture, integrations, data dependencies)
* Business rules, compliance requirements, or policy limitations
* Timeline, resource, or cost constraints

**5. Validate Understanding**
* Cross-check assumptions with stakeholders or real usage data
* Refine the problem statement to ensure alignment before proceeding

## Phase 2: Pathway Ideation & Generation

**1. Leverage Known Patterns and Past Experience**
* Recall similar problems solved previously
* Identify reusable architectural or process patterns
* Apply proven approaches where context aligns

**2. Explore External References and Benchmarks**
* Review how similar problems are solved in other systems, tools, or industries
* Identify standard practices, emerging patterns, or unconventional approaches

**3. Use First-Principles Thinking (when needed)**
* Break the problem down to fundamental components
* Reconstruct potential solutions without bias toward existing implementations

**4. Generate Multiple Viable Pathways**
* Define at least two distinct solution approaches (preferably more when complexity is high)
* Ensure each pathway represents a meaningfully different trade-off (e.g., speed vs. scalability, simplicity vs. flexibility)

**5. Maintain Breadth Before Convergence**
* Avoid prematurely converging on a single solution
* Keep options open until feasibility assessment is complete

## Phase 3: Feasibility Assessment & Recommendation

**1. Evaluation Criteria**
* Feasibility – Can this be realistically implemented within current constraints?
* Cost – Development effort, infrastructure, and operational cost
* Time to Deliver – Speed of implementation and time-to-value
* Scalability – Ability to handle future growth or increased load
* Maintainability – Ease of support, debugging, and future enhancements
* Risk – Technical uncertainty, dependency risks, and failure impact

**2. Comparative Analysis**
* Assess each pathway against the same criteria
* Highlight trade-offs explicitly rather than assuming an obvious "best" option

**3. Validation via Prototyping (when applicable)**
* Where uncertainty is high, build a minimal proof of concept (POC)
* Use POC results to validate assumptions and reduce risk

**4. Formulating the Recommendation**
* Select the most balanced option based on context and priorities
* Clearly articulate:
    - Why this option is preferred
    - What trade-offs are being accepted
    - Under what conditions another option might be better

**5. Stakeholder Communication**
* Present:
    - Problem summary
    - Evaluated options
    - Decision criteria
    - Final recommendation with rationale
* Tailor depth based on audience (technical vs. business)
