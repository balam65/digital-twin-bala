# Talent Discovery

## Objective
To identify high-potential talent by looking past superficial credentials and buzzwords, focusing instead on foundational understanding, learning agility, and structured problem-solving.

## Core Philosophy: Potential vs. Pedigree
* **Core Principle:** Specific skills can be learned; underlying thinking ability and mindset are much harder to teach.
* **What to Value Most:** 
  - Ability to deconstruct and solve unfamiliar problems.
  - Insatiable curiosity and willingness to learn.
  - Adaptability across disparate domains.
* **Role of Experience:** Relevant past experience is valuable primarily as an accelerator for execution and onboarding. It is *never* a substitute for depth of thinking.
* **Final Evaluation Bias:** Always favor a candidate with strong fundamentals and high potential over one with perfect resume alignment but shallow understanding.

## 1. Green Flags (High Potential Indicators)
Look for active signals that indicate rigorous thinking and problem ownership:

**Structured Thinking & Clarity**
- Breaks down complex problems deliberately before attempting solutions.
- Asks clarifying questions instead of jumping to conclusions.
- Can distill and explain highly complex concepts in simple terms.

**Foundational Understanding**
- Attempts to reason rather than relying on memorized design patterns.
- Willing to softly challenge given assumptions when appropriate.
- Can successfully adapt their knowledge to entirely unfamiliar scenarios.

**Learning Agility**
- Readily admits when they don't know something without hesitation or embarrassment.
- Immediately proposes a clear tactical approach to find the missing answer.
- Demonstrates genuine curiosity that extends beyond their immediate job requirements.

**Ownership & Accountability**
- Takes explicit responsibility for past decisions (both successes and failures).
- Explains the trade-offs of their past work rather than presenting it as "perfect."
- Shows a relentless bias toward solving the problem, rather than defending their ego.

**Signal of Depth Over Surface**
- Uses specific, contextual examples from real-world experience.
- Focuses on *why* a strategic or structural choice was made, not just *what* was implemented.
- Naturally connects operational concepts across disparate domains.

## 2. Red Flags (Risk & Weakness Indicators)
Actively monitor for subtle signs of shallow understanding, rigidity, or lack of extreme ownership:

**Superficial Knowledge**
- Throws around industry buzzwords but cannot explain the mechanics behind them.
- Provides textbook theoretical answers devoid of real-world operational context.
- Falters or dodges when presented with follow-up "how" or "why did you do it that way" questions.

**Lack of Problem Decomposition**
- Jumps straight into pitching solutions without fully understanding the underlying problem.
- Consistently struggles to break down ambiguous, multi-layered scenarios.
- Over-relies on templated or memorized patterns.

**Defensive or Ego-Driven Behavior**
- Becomes visibly defensive when a decision they made is professionally challenged.
- Actively avoids situations where they might have to admit a gap in their knowledge.
- Focuses more on being perceived as "right" than exploring superior alternative options.

**Low Ownership Signals**
- Instinctively blames external factors (management, other teams, legacy code) for failures without any internal reflection.
- Cannot clearly isolate and explain their specific contribution to past collaborative work.
- Skates past or outright avoids discussing mistakes and the subsequent trade-offs made.

**Rigidity**
- Expresses extreme difficulty or frustration when adapting to new contexts or shifting constraints.
- Shows an iron-clad dependence on specific tools, frameworks, or past solutions rather than adapting to the problem at hand.

## 3. The Litmus Test methodology
Rather than relying on fixed algorithm questions, utilize open-ended, slightly ambiguous problem scenarios that require deep structured thinking.

**Execution Strategy:**
1. Present a real-world system problem with intentionally incomplete information.
2. Observe their initial reaction and workflow.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- *Clarification:* Do they recognize the information gap and ask the right questions before solving?
- *Decomposition:* Can they accurately break the monolith problem down into workable components?
- *Trade-off Reasoning:* Do they actively discuss the pros and cons of the solution they are building?
- *Adaptability:* When an assumption they relied upon is suddenly changed by the interviewer, how well do they pivot?
